## The Paradigm Shift

For decades, mechanical engineers were bound by the constraints of subtractive manufacturing—milling, turning, and casting. **DFM (Design for Manufacturing)** was about simplifying geometry for a tool's path.

**DfAM (Design for Additive Manufacturing)** turns this on its head. Complexity is essentially free. 

## Key Strategies in DfAM

### Part Consolidation

The most powerful advantage of additive manufacturing is the ability to print complex assemblies as a single piece. 

-   **Reduces weight** by eliminating fasteners and flanges.
-   **Eliminates failure points** (seals and gaskets).
-   **Lowers assembly time** and logistical complexity.

### Lattice Structures

Lattice design allows for tailored material properties. By varying the thickness and cell type across a part, we can create:
-   **Stiffness gradients** for impact absorption.
-   **Porous regions** for fluid flow or biological osseointegration.

> "In DfAM, we don't just design the shape of the part; we design the behavior of the material itself."

## Practical Implementation

When moving a project to a DfAM workflow, the first step is **not** to simply 3D print an existing CAD file. Instead, we must start from the fundamental load requirements and allow the technology to dictate the form.

## Summary

Designing for Additive Manufacturing is not a tool; it is a mindset. For the modern engineer, it represents the ultimate freedom in functional expression.
